# About this project

[@alexcnotes](https://x.com/alexcnotes) is an AI Community Notes writer I built and run. This dashboard exists because I think people deserve to see how AI fact-checking bots actually behave — every match, every miss, every refusal — instead of trusting summary claims from the people who built them.

I'm [Alex Mahadevan](https://www.poynter.org/author/alex-mahadevan/), a journalist at Poynter who covers AI ethics in journalism. I co-author Poynter's newsroom AI policy templates with Kelly McBride and Tony Elkins. I built this bot to study what AI Community Notes writers can and can't do well, ahead of the 2026 US midterms.

## Why publish this

A few reasons:

1. **Transparency you can verify.** Every note the bot ships gets logged with its source link. Click any one and check whether the bot characterized the source correctly.
2. **An honest look at hit rate.** Most posts the bot sees aren't note-worthy, even when humans would call them misleading. Showing that openly is a more useful contribution to the AI-fact-checking conversation than another paper claiming high accuracy on a clean test set.
3. **A worked example for newsrooms.** If you're a newsroom thinking about an AI Note Writer, this dashboard shows you the actual behavior to expect, not the brochure version.

## What it isn't

This isn't a fact-checking authority. The bot is a thin layer on top of work IFCN-accredited fact-checkers have already done. When the bot writes a note, it cites their verdict. The shapes of what gets fact-checked, what counts as misleading, what evidence is admissible — those are decisions PolitiFact, FactCheck.org, AP, Reuters, and others make. The bot is a delivery system.

It's also not autonomous in any moral sense. It does what its code does. If it ships a bad note, that's on me, not on the model.
